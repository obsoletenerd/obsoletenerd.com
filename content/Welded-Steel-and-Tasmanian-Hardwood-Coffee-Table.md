We needed a new coffee table for our loungeroom and I wanted to finally start using some of my ridiculous collection of hardwood that has been sitting in the shed for years. I'm no wood worker, so doing anything other than basic stuff is a big ask, and I also don't really have the appropriate tools to do anything too fancy. I figured doing a steel frame with a hardwood top would be a good compromise, letting me feature the wood without needing any crazy tools while also taking advantage of the better tools I have for working with metal.

I was stuck on how to do the legs in a more interesting way than just normal straight legs, and my wife came up with the idea of angling them as pairs of V's on each corner. I loved the idea, but executing it is a whole different challenge considering the intricate/accurate angles required for them to all meet in the same plane and not leave the table wobbly.

I started by designing the frame in CAD so I could get it to calculate the appropriate angles, and then printed out a paper template to cut some scrap pieces of steel and just get an idea of how the angles connect so I can plan jigs and welding setup.

## Making the Tasmanian Hardwood Top

I have a huge collection of very old hardwood from Tasmania that I was given by my dad when he sold his farm, everything from massive beams to flooring slats to raw rough-cut pieces. I have the world's crappiest thicknesser, so any time I want to use these rough old pieces it's a massive investment in time/effort, but this project seemed worthy of that. I started off collecting a bunch of similarly sized rough-cut lengths that suited the plans, and throwing them through the thicknesser taking off ~0.25-0.5mm at a time (yes it's that crappy), putting them through repeatedly dozens of times until they were close enough to the final size I was after.

I then spent a solid couple days using a hand planer and hand saws to square them all up and even them all out to within a couple mm of each other. 

<img alt="Tasmanian Hardwood Before Thicknessing" src="{static}/images/2026-05-11-Hardwood-Planing-Before.jpg" width="49%"><img alt="Tasmanian Hardwood After Thicknessing" src="{static}/images/2026-05-11-Hardwood-Planing-After.jpg" width="49%">

<img alt="Tasmanian Hardwood After Planing" src="{static}/images/2026-05-11-Hardwood-Planed.jpg" width="98%">

When they were close enough to the final dimensions, I glued them up first in sections of a few pieces and then finally glued those sections together into the complete top. It's a long way from perfect considering I don't have a jointer or anything to truly square the edges up, nor do I want to spend the many days it would require to get them that close by hand, but I think the imperfections will actually suit the final look I'm going for with this table.

It then took many (many!) hours of planing then sanding through all the grits to get it smooth enough to a point where I was happy with it. Literal days of a few hours each afternoon to get it all smooth and square to final dimensions. This is where a thicknesser/jointer and table saw with a big sled really would have helped, but I don't really do enough woodwork to justify those yet.

## Fabricating the Steel Frame

After proving the concept with the paper templates and scrap metal, I used the laser cutter at my <a href="https://www.ballarathackerspace.org.au">local hackerspace</a> to create some MDF templates that would survive re-use and let me cut the 8 pieces required for all the legs. I absolutely love this method for cutting metal parts, and with laser cutters being so much more accessible lately it lets you get ridiculously accurate and replicable metal parts using nothing but cheap hand tools such as an angle grinder or cheap plasma torch. The templates are surprisingly strong too, and I have some templates that i've used dozens of times with a CNC torch and they're still perfectly usable.

I then spent the next few weekends hand filing, sanding, body-filling, sanding again, skim-coating, sanding again, priming, and sanding some more, to get the entire frame up to a level where I figured a few thick coats of paint could hide the rest. I severely underestimated this part of the build and it took *so* much longer than I ever expected, and I did lots of things wrong here that could've been done better/easier. That's how we learn though.

## Paint the Frame and Coating the Hardwood

Then it was time for paint. I used some heavy duty epoxy enamel matte-black paint for the frame, with the idea of just getting it done for now as by this point I was very very over this entire project. I do plan one day to re-paint it properly with my spray gun in something a bit nicer, but this works and is kid-proof for the time being. 

For the wood top I used a hardwood floor coating so that it's as hard-wearing as possible. Again this is easily redoable later, but I think this is a good choice for a coffee table being used by kids to draw on or spill food on.

## Finished Product

These aren't the best photos and they show the colour very oddly due to the dark loungeroom during winter (the wood is a lot more of a dark-brown and less orange in real life), but this is the final product. A "simple" coffee table that took 2 months of every spare minute I had, and pushed the limits of what I could do with my cheap tools. 

I'm overall very happy with it though and my family love it, so I consider it a win.
